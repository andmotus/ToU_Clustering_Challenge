# ETHICS_FRAMEWORK.md

## Purpose
This document outlines the ethical principles, risks, safeguards, and monitoring strategy for the Milan urban mobility clustering project. The aim is to ensure that the analysis supports **justice, equity, and sustainability goals**, rather than producing technically interesting but socially harmful categories.

The project uses anonymized, spatially aggregated telecom activity to identify functional urban zone types in Milan. Because clustering can shape how places are understood and prioritized, ethical reflection is necessary throughout the full workflow: problem framing, feature design, interpretation, communication, and potential use in decision-making.

---

## 1) Ethical Principles Guiding the Project

### A) Beneficence and Non-Maleficence
The project should create social value by supporting better, more context-sensitive urban mobility planning. At the same time, it must avoid harm.

**Beneficence**
- support more equitable and evidence-informed transport planning,
- help identify overlooked urban dynamics that may justify better service provision,
- contribute to more sustainable and place-sensitive interventions.

**Non-maleficence**
- avoid stigmatizing neighborhoods or labeling areas in harmful ways,
- avoid overclaiming what telecom activity can reveal,
- avoid enabling exclusionary or punitive uses of the results.

---

### B) Justice and Fairness

#### Distributive Justice
Benefits from the analysis should not flow only to already advantaged areas. Cluster insights should help reveal different mobility needs across the city, including those of areas that may be underserved or poorly represented by city-wide averages.

#### Procedural Justice
The process of interpretation and communication should be transparent and inclusive. Stakeholders should not simply receive cluster labels as finished truths; they should have opportunities to question and contextualize them.

#### Recognition Justice
The project should recognize that neighborhoods are socially complex and cannot be fully reduced to telecom activity patterns. Community perspectives and local knowledge are necessary to avoid one-dimensional interpretations.

---

### C) Autonomy and Consent
The project uses aggregated, anonymized data rather than individual-level personal records, which reduces direct privacy risks. Still, ethical responsibility remains.

- respect the fact that telecom-derived activity is socially sensitive,
- avoid implying that individuals have been directly profiled or behaviorally classified,
- communicate clearly that clustering applies to **spatial zones**, not named persons,
- treat community-level representation with care, since even aggregate categories can influence policy and perception.

---

## 2) Main Ethical Risks

### A) Reinforcing Existing Inequities
The underlying activity patterns may reflect existing inequalities in access, infrastructure, land use, or economic opportunity. If interpreted uncritically, clusters may normalize these inequalities rather than help address them.

**Example risk:**  
A low-activity area could be interpreted as “unimportant” rather than as potentially underserved or disconnected.

---

### B) Creating Harmful or Stigmatizing Categories
Cluster labels can become socially harmful if they are framed in deficit-based terms.

**Example risk:**  
Describing a cluster as a “problem area” or “inactive zone” rather than recognizing structural causes or contextual variation.

---

### C) Excluding Vulnerable Voices
The data capture aggregated telecom activity, but do not directly represent lived experience, accessibility barriers, or community priorities. Some groups may be less visible in telecom-derived signals than others.

**Example risk:**  
An area’s needs could be misunderstood if telecom intensity is taken as a full proxy for social importance or mobility justice.

---

### D) Algorithmic Determinism
There is a risk that cluster assignments are treated as fixed truths rather than as exploratory analytical tools.

**Example risk:**  
Using clusters to justify rigid planning decisions without acknowledging uncertainty, mixed-use areas, or temporal change.

---

### E) Misuse Beyond Original Scope
Results intended for planning insight could be misapplied in surveillance-oriented, exclusionary, or overly reductionist ways.

**Example risk:**  
Using cluster patterns to deprioritize certain neighborhoods or justify unequal service rather than improve responsiveness.

---

## 3) Ethical Safeguards

### A) Careful Framing and Language
The project will avoid deficit-based or stigmatizing labels.

**Safeguards**
- use neutral or functional labels such as *residential*, *commercial*, *mixed-use*, *nighttime activity*, or *transport-oriented*,
- avoid labels implying blame, deviance, or social deficiency,
- frame unusual patterns as signals for further inquiry, not as judgments.

---

### B) Transparency About What the Data Mean
The project will clearly state that telecom activity is a **proxy for urban dynamics**, not a direct measure of:
- individual mobility decisions,
- social worth,
- neighborhood need,
- or causal drivers of inequality.

**Safeguards**
- document data source and aggregation level,
- explain preprocessing decisions,
- communicate limitations in every major output,
- avoid claims that go beyond what the data can support.

---

### C) Human Interpretation and Contextual Validation
Clusters should not be interpreted in isolation.

**Safeguards**
- compare cluster outputs with known urban context where possible,
- use maps and temporal profiles to support cautious interpretation,
- treat results as one evidence layer among others, not as a stand-alone planning truth.

---

### D) Equity-Oriented Interpretation
The project will explicitly ask whether cluster differences reveal **unequal service needs or hidden variation**, rather than simply efficiency opportunities.

**Safeguards**
- examine whether certain cluster types align with potentially underserved urban patterns,
- discuss both efficiency and equity implications,
- avoid privileging only high-activity or high-visibility areas in interpretation.

---

### E) Avoiding Overprecision
Clustering often creates an illusion of exact boundaries, even when reality is fuzzy.

**Safeguards**
- emphasize that urban zones may be mixed-use and that cluster assignments simplify reality,
- consider model-based methods or soft interpretation if overlap appears substantial,
- avoid presenting clusters as permanent or universally valid.

---

## 4) Community Engagement Plan

Although this is a course project rather than a full participatory planning process, the ethical approach should still include a plan for community-oriented interpretation.

### A) Who should be engaged
- neighborhood and mobility advocacy groups,
- community organizations with local knowledge,
- urban planning and transport stakeholders who work closely with residents,
- researchers familiar with Milan’s socio-spatial context.

### B) How engagement should happen
- present findings in accessible language,
- use map-based outputs that allow local interpretation,
- invite feedback on whether cluster descriptions make sense in context,
- explicitly ask what the clustering may be missing.

### C) Purpose of engagement
- reduce the risk of misinterpretation,
- identify blind spots in the analysis,
- ensure that cluster narratives do not erase local realities,
- strengthen recognition justice by incorporating perspectives beyond the dataset itself.

Even if full stakeholder participation is limited in this project, documenting this engagement logic is important for responsible practice.

---

## 5) Ethical Decision Process for This Project

### Step 1: Stakeholder Impact Assessment
Before finalizing interpretation, ask:
- who could benefit from these findings?
- who could be harmed if these clusters are misread?
- which neighborhoods might be unfairly characterized?

### Step 2: Bias Audit
Review whether:
- cluster labels reproduce stereotypes,
- low-activity areas are interpreted unfairly,
- highly visible central areas receive disproportionate analytical attention.

### Step 3: Benefit-Risk Review
Assess whether:
- the expected planning value outweighs the risks of simplification,
- the results are likely to support more equitable understanding,
- safeguards are strong enough to reduce harmful misuse.

### Step 4: Communication Check
Before dissemination, confirm that:
- limitations are clearly stated,
- uncertainty is acknowledged,
- outputs do not imply deterministic or punitive uses.

---

## 6) Monitoring Strategy

Ethical responsibility continues after the analysis is complete. The project should include a monitoring logic for how findings are interpreted and potentially used.

### A) What to monitor
- whether clusters are being described in harmful or misleading ways,
- whether stakeholders focus only on efficiency and ignore equity implications,
- whether certain neighborhoods are unfairly singled out,
- whether users treat clusters as definitive facts instead of exploratory groupings.

### B) How to monitor
- maintain a written record of limitations and intended use,
- include cautionary notes in reports and presentations,
- track feedback from stakeholders on interpretability and fairness,
- revise labels or explanations if they create confusion or harm.

### C) Indicators of responsible use
The ethical goals of the project are being met if:
- clusters are used to support **more nuanced and place-sensitive planning**,
- discussions include both efficiency and equity,
- uncertainty and limitations remain visible,
- no cluster is treated as a moral or social judgment about an area or its residents.

---

## 7) Practical Safeguard Measures

To make the framework actionable, the project will aim to implement the following:

- **privacy-preserving handling** of already aggregated/anonymized data,
- **clear documentation** of all preprocessing and feature choices,
- **neutral and non-stigmatizing cluster naming**,
- **map-based and profile-based interpretation** rather than overreliance on labels alone,
- **explicit discussion of uncertainty and proxy limitations**,
- **equity-focused reflection** in the interpretation section,
- **stakeholder/community-oriented review logic** for contextual sense-checking.

---

## Summary
This project treats clustering as a tool for **supportive urban understanding**, not for labeling or ranking neighborhoods in a harmful way. The main ethical risks are misinterpretation, stigmatization, reinforcement of inequities, and overconfidence in proxy data. To address these risks, the project adopts safeguards centered on **transparency, careful language, contextual interpretation, community-aware engagement, and ongoing monitoring**. The ultimate ethical goal is to ensure that the clustering contributes to **justice, equity, and sustainability**, rather than merely producing technically valid groupings.
