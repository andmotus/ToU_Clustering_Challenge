# BENEFICIARY_IMPACT.md

## Purpose
This document identifies the main beneficiaries of the Milan urban mobility clustering project and explains the pathway through which clustering insights could create practical value. The goal is to ensure that the analysis is not only technically sound, but also relevant to **sustainability, equity, and real-world decision-making**.

---

## 1) Project Context

This project uses anonymized, spatially aggregated telecom activity from Milan to identify **functional urban zone types** based on temporal activity patterns. The intended contribution is a more detailed understanding of how different parts of the city are used over time, beyond what can be seen from city-wide averages or simple aggregate indicators.

The broader sustainability relevance lies in supporting **more targeted, place-sensitive mobility planning**, which may help improve transport efficiency, reduce congestion and emissions, and allocate public resources more fairly across neighborhoods.

---

## 2) Beneficiary Analysis

### A) Primary Beneficiaries

These are the actors most directly affected by decisions informed by the clustering results.

#### 1. Urban planners and transport authorities
These are the most immediate users of the analysis. Cluster insights can help them identify areas with different mobility and activity profiles, such as commuter-oriented zones, mixed-use areas, nightlife districts, or transport hubs.

**Potential benefit:**
- better targeting of transport services,
- more context-sensitive infrastructure planning,
- improved prioritization of interventions across the city.

#### 2. Residents and commuters
Although they are not the direct users of the model, they are important direct beneficiaries of better mobility decisions.

**Potential benefit:**
- improved access to transport,
- more responsive service planning,
- better alignment between urban infrastructure and actual activity patterns,
- potential improvements in safety, convenience, and travel efficiency.

#### 3. Neighborhoods with distinct but underserved mobility needs
Some areas may be overlooked when decisions rely only on city averages. A clustering-based typology can highlight neighborhoods with unusual or mixed-use patterns that require more tailored responses.

**Potential benefit:**
- more equitable recognition of local mobility needs,
- better visibility for areas that do not fit dominant planning assumptions,
- fairer resource allocation.

---

### B) Secondary Beneficiaries

These actors benefit indirectly through better urban systems and improved planning quality.

#### 1. The wider public / taxpayers
If city interventions become more targeted and efficient, public resources may be used more effectively.

**Potential benefit:**
- better value from transport and infrastructure spending,
- fewer misallocated or poorly targeted investments.

#### 2. Future residents and future generations
Sustainable mobility planning has long-term environmental and social effects.

**Potential benefit:**
- lower emissions from better transport management,
- more resilient and livable urban systems over time.

#### 3. Researchers and practitioners in other cities
If the workflow proves useful, it could be adapted to similar datasets in other urban contexts.

**Potential benefit:**
- transferable methodology,
- comparative urban analysis,
- stronger evidence base for data-driven mobility planning.

---

### C) Implementers / Change Agents

These are the actors most likely to translate clustering insights into action.

#### 1. Municipal policymakers
Can use cluster-based evidence to shape urban mobility policy, investment priorities, and planning strategies.

#### 2. Transport agencies and service planners
Can adapt service frequencies, routing priorities, or operational planning based on different urban activity profiles.

#### 3. Urban researchers and analysts
Can build on the clustering results to connect zone typologies with land use, accessibility, emissions, or social equity outcomes.

#### 4. Community advocates and local organizations
Can use cluster-informed evidence to argue for neighborhood-specific mobility improvements.

---

## 3) Impact Pathway

### Step 1: Analytical Outputs
The clustering analysis is expected to produce:

- cluster assignments for Milan grid cells,
- descriptive profiles of each cluster type,
- visualizations of temporal patterns,
- maps showing the spatial distribution of clusters,
- interpretation of likely functional zone categories.

These outputs transform raw telecom activity data into a more understandable representation of urban dynamics.

---

### Step 2: Intermediate Outcomes
If interpreted and communicated effectively, these outputs can support:

- improved awareness of differences between urban zones,
- more targeted planning discussions,
- better prioritization of transport and public-space interventions,
- stronger justification for differentiated policies rather than one-size-fits-all strategies,
- identification of underserved or atypical areas needing more attention.

At this stage, the value of clustering lies in helping stakeholders make **better-informed and more place-sensitive decisions**.

---

### Step 3: Long-Term Impacts
Over time, these better decisions could contribute to broader sustainability and equity outcomes such as:

- more efficient mobility systems,
- reduced congestion and transport-related emissions,
- fairer distribution of infrastructure and service improvements,
- improved accessibility and livability across neighborhoods,
- stronger urban resilience through more adaptive planning.

These long-term impacts are indirect and depend on implementation, but the clustering analysis can provide a useful evidence layer in that process.

---

## 4) Equity and Sustainability Considerations

A key reason this project matters is that **averages can hide spatial inequality**. Areas with distinct mobility rhythms may not receive appropriate attention if planning relies only on overall city trends. Clustering can help reveal hidden variation and support more equitable recognition of different neighborhood conditions.

However, the analysis must also be interpreted carefully:
- telecom activity is a **proxy** for urban dynamics, not a perfect measure of trips or social need,
- clustering should support, not replace, domain knowledge and local context,
- findings should not be used to oversimplify neighborhoods or justify exclusionary interventions.

For this reason, the project’s value lies not only in identifying patterns, but in making those patterns **useful, interpretable, and responsibly applied**.

---

## 5) How Value Will Be Evaluated

The project will be considered meaningful if the resulting clusters are:

- **interpretable** in terms of recognizable urban activity types,
- **useful** for discussing differentiated planning strategies,
- **spatially plausible** when mapped onto the city,
- **relevant** to sustainability and equity concerns rather than being only mathematically distinct.

In other words, the project should not only produce clusters—it should produce clusters that help explain something important about Milan’s urban mobility system and its implications for decision-making.

---

## Summary
The main beneficiaries of this project are **urban planners, transport authorities, residents, and neighborhoods whose mobility needs may be obscured in aggregate statistics**. The impact pathway runs from **clustered urban zone typologies**, to **better-informed planning and resource allocation**, to longer-term sustainability and equity outcomes such as improved accessibility, reduced emissions, and more responsive urban mobility policy.
